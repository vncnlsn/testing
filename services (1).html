What We Do

The land work that makes or breaks a project schedule.

Survey projects and infrastructure corridors fail on the land side more often than anywhere else. A title chain with a gap, a landowner who wasn't contacted properly, a permit that stalled because the documentation wasn't complete — these are the problems APS Land was built to prevent.

We handle the three areas where that failure most commonly happens: researching and documenting ownership, negotiating and securing landowner agreements, and coordinating the agency permitting that gives field crews legal access to the ground. We also offer the full complement of services that a complete project requires — GIS, 2D/3D design, leasing, cost tracking — so your team isn't managing multiple land contractors when one will do.

01 — Core Service

Title Research &
Property Records

Ownership of land in energy-producing states is rarely simple. Surface rights and mineral rights are often held separately, by different parties, sometimes across multiple generations of estate distribution. Easements from pipelines laid decades ago may or may not be on record where you'd expect to find them.

We do the courthouse work — patent-to-present title research, mineral rights chain of title, surface ownership verification — and we do it in person when that's what it takes. Not every county clerk's office has digitized what you need.

The result is documentation your legal and operations teams can actually rely on, with identified problem areas flagged before they become disputes or delays.

What this covers

  • Cursory and patent-to-present title research
  • Surface & mineral rights separation and identification
  • Easement and ROW record research
  • Estate and trust ownership documentation
  • County courthouse record retrieval
  • GIS parcel mapping and integration
  • Mineral and service data management

02 — Core Service

Landowner Relations &
Access Agreements

There is a version of this work that treats landowners as obstacles to route around. That version produces signed permits and landowners who remember it — and tell their neighbors. We've spent four decades doing the other version.

Our agents go in with the project details in plain language, the compensation terms documented, and the authority to answer questions honestly. When a landowner has concerns about crop damage, livestock access, or what happens when crews leave — we address those concerns before asking for a signature.

The agreements we produce include specific conditions, restoration commitments, and compensation structures that are accurate to what the client has authorized. When field crews deviate from what the agreement says, that's something we track and address — not something landowners have to fight about after the fact.

What this covers

  • Landowner contact and initial outreach
  • Project explanation and Q&A
  • Compensation negotiation
  • Seismic options & right-of-way acquisition
  • Oil & gas leasing
  • Access agreement drafting and execution
  • Restoration commitment documentation
  • IRS 1099 reporting for landowner payments

03 — Core Service

Permit Acquisition &
Regulatory Coordination

Permitting is the step that translates landowner agreements and title research into legal field access. It's also where projects stall when the documentation coming in is incomplete or when no one has a relationship with the agency reviewer on the other end.

We manage 2D and 3D permit acquisition, micro-seismic and VSP permitting, and state agency coordination — and we do it on either a turnkey or day-rate basis, depending on how your project is structured. Permit cost tracking and IRS reporting are included, not billed as add-ons.

Our project management approach is built around keeping your operations team informed. Timeline status, cost reporting, and any flag that appears during the permitting process get communicated before they become schedule issues.

What this covers

  • 2D and 3D seismic permitting
  • Micro-seismic & VSP permitting
  • Turnkey or day-rate contract structures
  • State and federal agency coordination
  • Regulatory and environmental permitting
  • Permit cost tracking and reporting
  • Project management and timeline communication
  • Survey, drilling & recording crew coordination

For Landowners

If APS Land contacts you about your property — here's what to expect.

Geophysical surveys and energy infrastructure projects involve your land and your rights. This is what the process looks like from your side of it.

STEP 01

Initial Contact

You'll receive a letter, a phone call, or a visit from one of our agents. They'll introduce themselves, explain who they represent, and describe the project in plain terms — what type of work is being done, when, and on what part of your property. You are not obligated to agree to anything at this stage.

STEP 02

Project Explanation & Your Questions

Our agent will walk through what the survey crew will actually do: what equipment they use, what routes they'll travel, what impact (if any) there will be on crops, fencing, or pasture. Ask what you want to know. You should have a complete picture before any paperwork is discussed.

STEP 03

Compensation & Agreement Terms

If you're willing to allow access, we negotiate compensation and draft an agreement that reflects what was discussed — not what's easiest for the client. The agreement specifies what crews are authorized to do, what they're not, and what happens if there's damage. You receive a signed copy.

STEP 04

Before Crews Arrive

We notify you before crews come onto your property. You have a direct contact name and number. If crews do something they weren't authorized to do — or if something goes wrong — call us. We are responsible for what happens on your land under the agreement we made.

STEP 05

After the Work Is Done

Restoration requirements in the agreement are followed, not suggested. Gates left open get addressed. Ruts get repaired. Your compensation is paid accurately and on time. If there's a dispute, our records of what the agreement said and what happened in the field are there to resolve it.

YOUR RIGHT

You can always say no.

Nothing about the process obligates you to grant access to your property. We work to reach agreements that make sense for both sides. If the terms don't work for you, we document that and move on. Your rights as a landowner are not something we work around.
